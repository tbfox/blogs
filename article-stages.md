That final stage is this. The article you are reading now is the finished product — but every earlier version existed in public, labeled for what it was.

That is the whole idea. Let me explain it.

## The Problem With AI-Assisted Writing

AI can speed up writing dramatically. That is not a controversial claim. What is less discussed is what you give up if you use it carelessly: the sense that a real person thought carefully about what they were saying.

The answer is not to avoid AI. The answer is to be honest about where it is and where it is not — and to make sure a human is genuinely in the loop, not just rubber-stamping output.

My solution is a staged publishing workflow. Each stage is committed separately, labeled clearly, and visible to readers who want to see it. The AI does scaffolding. I do judgment. Nothing ships without me reading every word.

## Stage 1: Stub

The article exists before the content does. I publish a placeholder — a title, maybe a sentence or two, and a clear message that says something like: "This post is a stub. Content is coming."

That is it. No filler. No fake depth.

The point is to stake a claim on a topic before the work begins. It signals intent without pretending something is finished. And it sets an honest baseline: the reader knows, from the very first moment, exactly where the article stands.

## Stage 2: Raw Recording

When I am ready to develop the topic, I record myself talking about it. No script. No editing. Just me thinking out loud for however long it takes to get the ideas out.

The transcript goes up as-is.

The roughness is intentional. This is real thought in its most unfiltered form. Readers who encounter an article at this stage are seeing my actual thinking process — the half-formed ideas, the circling back, the instincts before they become arguments. It removes the blank-page problem and gives everything that follows something genuine to build from.

## Stage 3: AI-Generated Plan (Pre-Human Audit)

An AI agent takes the raw transcript and produces a structured plan: sections, bullet points, gaps it noticed, things worth addressing. This is published as-is, labeled explicitly as AI output before any human has touched it.

This stage does the scaffolding. It takes unstructured thought and imposes an outline on it. It surfaces redundancies and missing pieces I might not have caught in the moment.

But it is clearly labeled as pre-human audit. Readers know this step happened. They can see exactly how much of the structure is AI-derived, because it is sitting right there.

## Stage 4: Human-Audited Plan

This is the most important stage in the whole process.

I go through the AI-generated plan word by word. I revise sections that are off. I cut things that do not belong. I add things the AI missed. I make editorial decisions about what the article is actually arguing and how it should be organized.

The result is a revised plan that reflects my judgment, not just the AI's first pass at structure.

This stage is the clearest evidence that the workflow is not just AI slop. Every line has been read and considered. The AI gave me something to react to; I did the reacting. That distinction matters.

## Stage 5: AI-Generated Draft

From the human-audited plan, an AI agent writes a full prose draft of the article.

"Finished" is in air quotes here. This is a scaffold, not a final product. The draft translates a structured outline into readable sentences and paragraphs, quickly, so I have something concrete to work with rather than starting from a blank page.

This stage is also labeled clearly when readers encounter it. The prose is auto-generated. Readers know that. The point is not to hide it — the point is to use it as a starting point for something better.

## Stage 6: Human-Reviewed Final Article

I read the AI draft in full. I revise it. I sign off on it. Then it publishes as the final article.

Every word in the finished piece has been read by me. The volume of articles this workflow enables does not come at the cost of human oversight — it ends here, every time. That is the commitment.

## Why Publish Every Stage?

The short answer: readers deserve to know how content is made.

Publishing every stage makes the AI involvement explicit rather than hidden. It demonstrates that there is a human in the loop — not as a talking point, but as a verifiable fact. If you are technically inclined and want to trace exactly what changed between stages, the commit history is there. Every stage is committed separately. You can see the diff between the AI-generated plan and the human-audited plan. You can see what I changed in the final draft.

There is no special viewer built in. If you can read a git log, you can trace every change. I am not hiding anything, and I am also not hand-holding.

The goal of this workflow is more articles, not worse articles. AI handles structure and prose scaffolding. I handle judgment, voice, and final approval. The staged approach keeps those roles honest — on my end and for readers.
